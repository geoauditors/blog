5 Things You Can Fix Today to Improve Your AI Visibility

March 27, 2026
Quick WinsAI VisibilityGetting Started

You don’t need a six-month SEO overhaul to start showing up in AI search results. The gap between invisible and visible is often a handful of straightforward fixes — things that take minutes to check and less than an hour to act on.

AI-powered search engines like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google’s AI Overviews pull information differently than traditional search. They look for structured, factual, clearly attributed content. They weigh cross-platform brand signals. And they respect (or are blocked by) technical settings that many website owners have never thought about.

Here are five changes you can make today — each one achievable by a non-technical person or a quick request to your developer — that will meaningfully improve how AI systems see and cite your business.

What to do: Open a browser and go to yoursite.com/robots.txt. This is a plain text file that tells web crawlers what they can and cannot access on your site. Look for a line that says Disallow: / — that means you are telling every crawler, including AI systems, to stay away from your entire site.

If you see that blanket block, ask your developer to add specific exceptions for AI crawlers. The three most important ones right now are GPTBot (OpenAI), ClaudeBot (Anthropic), and PerplexityBot (Perplexity). A simple set of rules that allows these bots to index your public pages is all it takes.

Why it matters: AI models can only reference what they can read. If your robots.txt blocks AI crawlers, you are opting out of AI search entirely. Many sites inherited restrictive robots.txt files from years ago, before AI search existed, and never updated them. This is the single most common reason businesses are invisible to AI.

How long it takes: Two minutes to check. Five minutes for your developer to update.

2. Add an FAQ Section to Your Homepage

What to do: Write down the five to ten questions your customers actually ask — the ones your sales team hears every week, the ones that fill your support inbox. Then write a clear, one-paragraph answer for each. Add these to your homepage or a dedicated FAQ page in a simple question-and-answer format.

Don’t overthink the writing. Use the same language your customers use when they ask. If they say “How much does it cost?” don’t rewrite it as “Investment and pricing structure overview.” Keep the answers factual and specific. A good FAQ answer is one paragraph that directly addresses the question without filler.

Why it matters: AI systems are built to answer questions. When someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity a question about your industry, those systems look for content that is already structured as a direct answer. FAQ pairs are the closest match to how AI models process and retrieve information. A well-written FAQ section is essentially pre-formatted for AI citation.

How long it takes: Thirty to forty-five minutes to write. Another fifteen minutes to add it to your site, or less if your CMS has an FAQ block or widget.

3. Add Organization Schema to Your Site

What to do: Schema markup is a standardized way of telling search engines and AI systems structured facts about your business. Organization schema specifically communicates your company name, website URL, logo, and links to your official social media profiles, Wikipedia page, and other authoritative presences.

Most content management systems — WordPress, Squarespace, Wix, Shopify — have plugins or built-in settings for adding schema markup without touching code. Search for “schema markup plugin” plus your platform name, and you will find options. The key fields to fill in are your organization name, URL, logo image, and sameAs links pointing to your LinkedIn, Twitter, Facebook, and any other official profiles.

Why it matters: AI systems use schema markup as a trust signal. When an AI encounters Organization schema on your site, it can confidently connect your website to your social profiles, your Wikipedia entry, and your other official presences. This cross-referencing is how AI models verify that a business is real, established, and authoritative. Without schema, AI has to guess those connections — and it often guesses wrong or skips you entirely.

How long it takes: Fifteen to twenty minutes with a plugin. You will need your logo URL and links to your official social profiles on hand.

4. Write a Clear Meta Description for Every Key Page

What to do: A meta description is the short summary that appears below your page title in search results. Open your CMS and check the meta descriptions for your homepage, your main service or product pages, and your about page. If any of them are blank, generic, or stuffed with marketing buzzwords, rewrite them.

A good meta description is under 160 characters, states what the page is about in plain language, and includes a specific fact or differentiator. Compare these two examples:

  • Weak: “We offer world-class solutions for all your business needs. Contact us today!”
  • Strong: “Portland-based accounting firm serving small businesses since 2012. Tax prep, bookkeeping, and payroll for companies with 5-50 employees.”

The second one tells an AI system exactly what this business is, where it operates, and who it serves. That specificity is what gets you cited.

Why it matters: When AI systems summarize or recommend businesses, they lean heavily on meta descriptions as a quick, reliable source of what a page is about. A vague or missing meta description forces the AI to scan your full page content and make its own summary — which may miss your key selling points or skip your page altogether in favor of a competitor with a clearer description.

How long it takes: Five to ten minutes per page. Budget thirty minutes to cover your five most important pages.

5. Establish Your Presence on Wikipedia, LinkedIn, and Google Business

What to do: This one has three parts, and you may already have some of them covered.

LinkedIn: If your company does not have a LinkedIn company page, create one. If you have one but it is sparse, fill it out completely — description, industry, company size, headquarters, website link, and a recent post or two. LinkedIn company pages are one of the strongest brand authority signals for AI systems.

Google Business Profile: Claim or create your Google Business profile at business.google.com. Fill in every available field: hours, services, photos, description, and categories. Respond to reviews. A complete Google Business profile tells AI systems that you are an active, legitimate business at a real location.

Wikipedia: This is the hardest of the three, and not every business qualifies. Wikipedia requires that your company has been covered by independent, reliable sources. If your business has been featured in news articles, industry publications, or other third-party sources, you may be eligible. Do not write your own Wikipedia article — it will likely be flagged and removed. Instead, check if your company meets Wikipedia’s notability guidelines, and if it does, consider hiring an experienced Wikipedia editor to draft an article.

Why it matters: AI models are trained on and actively reference data from major platforms. When an AI system encounters your brand mentioned consistently across Wikipedia, LinkedIn, and Google Business — with matching details — it builds a strong internal model of your business as trustworthy and established. This cross-platform consistency is what researchers call “brand authority,” and it directly influences whether AI recommends you or your competitor.

How long it takes: LinkedIn and Google Business profiles each take about thirty minutes to set up properly. Wikipedia is a longer-term project, but even checking your eligibility takes only fifteen minutes.

These Five Changes Add Up

None of these fixes requires a redesign, a new content strategy, or a major budget. They are maintenance tasks — the digital equivalent of making sure your storefront sign is visible, your door is unlocked, and your phone number is listed correctly.

But together, they address the core reasons most businesses are invisible to AI search: blocked access, unstructured content, missing identity signals, vague descriptions, and thin cross-platform presence. Fix all five, and you have covered the fundamentals that AI systems need to find you, understand you, and recommend you.

The shift to AI-powered search is not coming — it is here. The businesses that show up in AI results today are not necessarily the biggest or the best. They are the ones that made it easy for AI to understand who they are and what they do. These five changes are where that starts.
